Electrolytes & Minerals

Electrolytes and minerals are both essential for optimal body function and often overlap — but they serve distinct purposes. Understanding the difference helps you target the right nutrients at the right time.

The Core Distinction

Charged minerals

Electrolytes

Electrolytes are minerals that carry an electrical charge when dissolved in water. This charge is what makes them uniquely capable of conducting nerve impulses and driving fluid movement across cell membranes.

  • Hydration — regulate fluid balance inside and outside cells
  • Muscle function — drive proper contractions, including the heart
  • Nerve signalling — facilitate communication between neurons
  • pH balance — maintain the acid-base balance of the blood

Inorganic essentials

Minerals

Minerals are inorganic substances the body cannot synthesise — they must come from food. Some minerals are electrolytes, but others serve structural or catalytic roles that have nothing to do with electrical charge.

  • Bone health — calcium and phosphorus form bone and tooth structure
  • Oxygen transport — iron is vital for haemoglobin in red blood cells
  • Enzyme activity — many minerals act as cofactors that activate enzymes
  • Immune function — zinc and selenium support immune defence

Key Electrolytes — Roles & Sources

Each electrolyte has a primary role and distinct food sources. Deficiency in any one can have cascading effects across multiple systems.

Na⁺

Sodium

The main extracellular electrolyte. Controls blood volume, blood pressure, and the osmotic pressure that moves water in and out of cells.

  • Table salt, olives, pickles, canned foods, soy sauce
  • Lost heavily in sweat — the first electrolyte to replenish after exercise
fluid balanceblood pressure
K⁺

Potassium

The primary intracellular electrolyte. Works opposite to sodium to regulate cell membrane potential, heart rhythm, and muscle contraction.

  • Bananas, avocado, sweet potato, spinach, beans, lentils
  • Low intake linked to hypertension and muscle cramps
heart rhythmmuscle
Ca²⁺

Calcium

Most abundant mineral in the body — 99% stored in bones and teeth. The remaining 1% is critical for muscle contraction, nerve firing, and blood clotting.

  • Dairy, fortified plant milks, sardines, kale, broccoli, tofu
  • Requires vitamin D for absorption
bone healthnerve firing
Mg²⁺

Magnesium

Involved in over 300 enzyme reactions. Supports ATP energy production, protein synthesis, nerve function, and blood sugar control.

  • Dark chocolate, nuts, seeds, legumes, whole grains, leafy greens
  • Commonly low in modern diets; depletion worsens anxiety and sleep
energysleep
Cl⁻

Chloride

Pairs with sodium to maintain fluid balance and is a key component of stomach acid (HCl), which is essential for digestion and pathogen defence.

  • Table salt (sodium chloride), seaweed, rye, tomatoes, celery
  • Rarely deficient on its own; follows sodium losses
digestionfluid balance
HCO₃⁻ / PO₄³⁻

Bicarbonate & Phosphate

Bicarbonate is the blood's primary pH buffer, neutralising excess acid. Phosphate supports bone mineralisation, energy currency (ATP), and DNA structure.

  • Bicarbonate: produced by the kidneys and lungs endogenously
  • Phosphate: meat, fish, dairy, nuts, seeds, legumes

When Each Matters Most

During intense exercise or illness: Electrolyte replenishment — especially sodium, potassium, and magnesium — is the immediate priority. These are lost rapidly through sweat, vomiting, or diarrhoea and must be replaced to restore nerve and muscle function.

For long-term health: A balanced intake of all essential minerals (both electrolytes and non-electrolytes) from whole foods supports bone density, immune resilience, hormonal balance, and cellular energy over time.

How to Balance Both

1

Diet First

Prioritise whole foods naturally rich in both categories: leafy greens, nuts, seeds, legumes, fruits, quality dairy or fortified alternatives, and lean meats or fish. Variety across food groups covers most needs without supplementation.

2

Targeted Supplementation

Consider electrolyte drinks or tablets after heavy sweating or prolonged activity. For general mineral gaps — especially iron, magnesium, zinc, or vitamin D (which affects calcium absorption) — supplement only where a deficiency is suspected or confirmed, ideally with testing.

3

Account for Individual Needs

Specific conditions alter requirements significantly. Kidney disease may require limiting potassium and phosphate. High blood pressure may benefit from sodium reduction. Pregnancy increases iron and folate needs. Work with a clinician if any of these apply.

Key takeaway. Neither electrolytes nor minerals are "more important" — they serve overlapping but distinct functions. Electrolytes are the immediate regulators of hydration, nerve firing, and muscle contraction. Broader minerals underpin long-term structure, immunity, and metabolism. A diet built around whole, varied foods covers most of both.
