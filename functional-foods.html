Functional Foods

Foods associated with anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, antimicrobial, and gut-supportive effects — organized by their main compounds, how strong the evidence is, simple ways to use them, and cautions to keep in mind.

For education, not treatment. These foods may support general health, but they do not diagnose, treat, or cure disease. Evidence varies by food, compound, dose, and individual health condition. "Antimicrobial" here means studied for antimicrobial activity — these foods are not substitutes for antibiotics or medical care. If you take medication, are pregnant, or have a health condition, check with a clinician before making big changes.

How to read the evidence strength

Strong

Consistent human trials or reviews, often as part of a broader dietary pattern (e.g. Mediterranean eating).

Moderate

Some human studies point in a helpful direction, though results vary and more research is useful.

Early

Limited or early human evidence — promising but not yet well established.

Lab-based

Mostly lab or animal studies. Real-world food amounts may be far lower than what was tested.

Foods to limit

Cutting these back tends to matter as much as adding "good" foods:

  • Ultra-processed foods
  • Refined sugar and sugary drinks
  • Fried foods and refined seed-oil heavy meals
  • Processed meats
  • Excess alcohol

Lifestyle factors

Food works alongside the basics — not instead of them:

  • Sleep and recovery
  • Hydration
  • Regular movement and exercise
  • Stress reduction
  • Sunlight / vitamin D status
  • Overall gut health and fibre variety
