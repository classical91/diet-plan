Studies: Calming Foods, Drinks, Spices & Herbs

A curated collection of real human trials on what we eat and drink to settle the nervous system — herbs, teas, spices, and foods shown to lower anxiety, stress, or the stress hormone cortisol. Every card cites a randomized controlled trial, systematic review, or meta-analysis you can open and read.

How to read this. "Calming" usually means lower self-reported anxiety/stress, lower cortisol (the stress hormone), or a shift toward the parasympathetic "rest-and-digest" state. The strongest evidence here is a meta-analysis (many trials pooled); single RCTs are weaker but still informative. These are educational summaries, not medical advice — herbs and extracts can interact with medications (sedatives, antidepressants, blood thinners) and with pregnancy. For persistent anxiety, panic, or insomnia, see a clinician. For everyday levers like breathing, movement, and sleep, see Calm the Nervous System.

Calming Herbs

Traditional "nervine" herbs that have made it into modern randomized trials. Most work gently and gradually rather than like a sedative — useful as a support layer, not a replacement for treatment.

Chamomile

Chamomile for anxiety

Mao et al. — long-term randomized controlled trial in generalized anxiety disorder, Phytomedicine (2016); building on Amsterdam et al., J. Clin. Psychopharmacology (2009).

A standardized German chamomile extract (1,500 mg/day) significantly reduced moderate-to-severe GAD symptoms versus placebo, and long-term use was safe and well tolerated (though it did not significantly cut relapse rates).

  • Brewed as a tea it is far weaker than the concentrated extract used in trials
  • Can add to the effect of sedatives; caution with ragweed allergy

Lavender

Oral lavender oil (Silexan)

Möller et al. — meta-analysis of randomized, placebo-controlled trials of Silexan in subthreshold anxiety, PMC (2019).

Pooling trials of 80 mg/day oral lavender-oil capsules over 10 weeks, Silexan was superior to placebo at lowering anxiety (HAM-A scores) and improved sleep without sedation or dependence.

  • Findings apply to the standardized oral capsule, not tea or essential-oil drops
  • Mild burping/lavender taste is the most common side effect

Lemon Balm

Lemon balm (Melissa officinalis)

Ghazizadeh et al. — systematic review & meta-analysis of clinical trials, Phytotherapy Research (2021).

Across randomized trials, lemon balm significantly improved anxiety and depression scores versus placebo, with effects most evident in acute, short-term use (results vary, so read with caution).

  • One cardiac-surgery RCT found 1.5 g/day of dried leaf cut anxiety and improved sleep
  • Pleasant as a tea; often paired with valerian in calming blends

Passionflower

Passionflower vs. a sedative

Akhondzadeh et al. — double-blind randomized trial of Passiflora incarnata vs. oxazepam in GAD, J. Clinical Pharmacy & Therapeutics (2001).

Over 4 weeks, passionflower extract was as effective as the benzodiazepine oxazepam for generalized anxiety, with fewer problems with daytime job performance than the drug.

  • A small pilot trial, but repeatedly supported for pre-surgery and dental anxiety
  • Tends to calm without the strong drowsiness of sedatives

Valerian

Valerian root for sleep

Shinjyo, Waddell & Green — systematic review & meta-analysis, J. Evidence-Based Integrative Medicine (2020).

Reviewing 60 studies, whole valerian root improved subjective sleep quality and showed anxiolytic effects, with no severe adverse events across ages 7–80 (evidence is stronger for sleep than for anxiety).

  • Quality varies a lot between products; whole-root extracts performed best
  • Best known as a bedtime herb; can compound other sedatives

Holy Basil / Tulsi

Tulsi lowers cortisol

Lopresti et al. — randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial of an Ocimum tenuiflorum extract (Holixer™), PMC (2022).

In stressed adults, holy basil extract was linked to lower salivary cortisol and amylase (stress markers) and improved stress, mood, and sleep over the trial.

  • A revered Ayurvedic adaptogen; also drunk as tulsi tea
  • May lower blood sugar — monitor if diabetic or on glucose-lowering meds

Kava

Kava (Piper methysticum)

Witte et al. — meta-analysis of the WS1490 kava extract in non-psychotic anxiety, Phytotherapy Research (2005); Sarris et al. KADSS crossover RCT, Psychopharmacology (2009).

Kava extract significantly reduced anxiety versus placebo (meta-analytic odds ratio ≈ 3.3), and an aqueous-extract RCT lowered Hamilton Anxiety scores — one of the stronger herbal anxiety signals.

  • Liver-safety caution: rare but serious liver injury has been reported — avoid with alcohol, liver disease, or hepatotoxic drugs
  • Use water-based ("noble" cultivar) extracts; not for daily long-term use without oversight

Rhodiola

Rhodiola rosea for stress & burnout

Hung, Perry & Ernst — systematic review of RCTs, Phytomedicine (2011); Kasper & Dienel review, Neuropsychiatric Disease & Treatment (2017).

Across randomized trials, Rhodiola reduced stress, fatigue, and burnout symptoms (acting via the HPA stress axis), with some improvements appearing within 3 days — though trials are small and exploratory.

  • An adaptogen taken as a standardized root extract, usually in the morning
  • Can feel mildly stimulating; best earlier in the day

Spices & Adaptogens

Culinary spices that double as mood support in the research. Doses in trials are usually higher and more standardized than what you would sprinkle on food.

Saffron

Saffron for mood & anxiety

Meta-analysis of RCTs comparing saffron with SSRIs for depression and anxiety, Nutrition Reviews (2024).

Across randomized trials, saffron was comparable to SSRI antidepressants at reducing depressive and anxiety symptoms, while causing fewer adverse events — positioning it as a potential gentler option.

  • Trial doses are around 30 mg/day of standardized extract
  • High doses can be unsafe (and the spice itself is costly); avoid in pregnancy

Turmeric / Curcumin

Curcumin for anxiety

Wang et al. — systematic review & meta-analysis of 8 randomized controlled trials (567 participants), Clinical Nutrition ESPEN (2024).

Pooling the trials, curcumin supplementation significantly reduced anxiety symptoms versus control; companion meta-analyses also show lower depressive symptoms, though the evidence base is still small.

  • Curcumin is poorly absorbed — trials use concentrated extracts (often with piperine/black pepper), not culinary turmeric
  • Can thin the blood and lower blood sugar; check before surgery or with medication

Teas & Drinks

What is in the cup matters. The most consistent calming compound in everyday drinks is L-theanine, the amino acid that gives green tea its smooth, "calm-alert" character.

Green Tea

L-theanine (green-tea amino acid)

Hidese et al. — 4-week randomized controlled trial, 200 mg/day, Nutrients (2019); plus crossover trials on acute stress.

L-theanine reduced stress-related symptoms, anxiety, and sleep-latency in healthy adults, and acutely blunted the cortisol response to a mental stressor — producing calm without sedation.

  • Naturally in green tea; pairs with its caffeine for steadier, jitter-free focus
  • Effect is gentle and most noticeable when baseline stress is higher

Foods

Whole foods move the needle more slowly than extracts, but two food patterns have randomized-trial support for stress and mood: polyphenol-rich dark chocolate, and a fermented-food-rich "psychobiotic" diet working through the gut.

Dark Chocolate

Polyphenol-rich dark chocolate

Tsang et al. — randomized study on polyphenol-rich dark chocolate, salivary cortisol & mood, Antioxidants (2019).

Dark chocolate high in cocoa polyphenols was associated with lower salivary cortisol and improved mood; separate trials show higher polyphenol doses (~500 mg) lift positive mood more than low doses.

  • Choose high-cocoa (70%+) dark chocolate — the polyphenols, not the sugar, are the active part
  • A small daily square is the realistic "dose"; calories still count

Fermented Foods

A "psychobiotic" diet

Berding et al. — randomized controlled trial of a fermented- & fibre-rich diet, Molecular Psychiatry (2023).

A 4-week diet rich in fermented foods and prebiotic fibre lowered perceived stress in healthy adults (the more closely people followed it, the larger the drop) by reshaping the gut microbiome — the gut–brain axis in action.

  • Think yogurt, kefir, sauerkraut, kimchi, miso, plus onions, leeks, oats, and legumes
  • Food-first and low-risk; effects build over weeks, not minutes

Foods & Drinks for Sleep

A calm nervous system and good sleep feed each other. Two whole foods have randomized-trial support for falling asleep faster and sleeping more soundly — gentle, food-first options with no real downside.

Tart Cherry Juice

Montmorency tart cherry

Howatson et al. — randomized crossover trial on melatonin & sleep, European J. of Nutrition (2012); Losso et al. RCT in older adults with insomnia (2018).

Tart cherry juice raised melatonin levels and improved sleep time and efficiency; in adults with insomnia it added roughly 84 minutes of sleep versus placebo.

  • A natural source of melatonin and tryptophan support
  • Watch the sugar load — concentrate diluted in water is a common form

Kiwifruit

Two kiwis before bed

Lin et al. — trial in adults with sleep problems, Asia Pacific J. of Clinical Nutrition (2011).

Eating 2 kiwifruit an hour before bed for 4 weeks cut time-to-fall-asleep by ~35% and increased total sleep time and efficiency — credited to kiwi's serotonin and antioxidants.

  • Simple, whole-food bedtime snack; also a good fibre + vitamin C source
  • Single-arm design, so weaker evidence — but low-risk to try

The Honest Caveats

Read the evidence carefully. Many of these trials are small, short, or run with industry funding, and a few meta-analyses note high variability or publication bias (chamomile and valerian for anxiety, in particular). The effects are generally modest — real, but milder than therapy or medication. "Natural" does not mean "harmless": herbs and spices can interact with antidepressants, sedatives, blood thinners, and blood-sugar or thyroid medication, and several are not recommended in pregnancy. Treat this page as a map of the research, and make medication and diagnosis decisions with a clinician.
