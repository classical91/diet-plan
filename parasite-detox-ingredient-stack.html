Ingredient stack

What those ingredients are usually for

This breaks down the common parasite-cleanse formula pattern: antiparasitic herbs first, binders second. The herbs are the “active cleanse” side; the binders are more like gut adsorbents, not parasite killers.

🌿

Antiparasitic herb trio

Clove, wormwood, and black walnut hull are the classic herbal-cleanse stack. They are commonly marketed for worms, eggs, microbes, and digestive cleansing, but human evidence varies and strong extracts need caution.

CloveAromatic spice. Often used in formulas because of strong plant compounds. Food-level clove is different from clove oil or high-dose capsules.
WormwoodTraditional bitter herb. Stronger and more caution-heavy. Not casual for pregnancy, seizure history, kidney/liver issues, or medication use.
Black walnut hullTraditional cleansing herb. Avoid with tree-nut allergy. Better treated as practitioner-guided rather than random daily use.

Binder trio

Zeolite, bentonite clay, and activated charcoal are usually used as binders. They may adsorb substances in the gut, but they do not “hunt parasites.” The main concern is that they can also bind helpful things.

Activated charcoalPowerful adsorbent. Can reduce absorption of medications and supplements, so timing matters.
Bentonite clayClay binder. Main concerns: constipation, contamination risk, and taking it too close to medicines or nutrients.
ZeoliteMineral binder. Often marketed for heavy metals/toxins, but product purity and proof matter. Look for third-party testing.

Cleanse herbs

Binder support

Binders are usually taken away from medications, supplements, and meals because they can reduce absorption.

3

Exit route

Hydration, bowel regularity, and electrolytes matter. A binder stack can backfire if it causes constipation.
