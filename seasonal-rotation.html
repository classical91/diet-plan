Reference Guide

Seasonal Rotation

A simple Ayurveda-inspired way to rotate teas, herbs, spices, foods, and routines with the season instead of using the same things all year.

Season changes → body needs change → rotate support.

Teas are only one piece. The bigger idea is seasonal rotation: adjusting herbs, foods, drinks, spices, oils, meal heaviness, and daily routines to match climate, digestion, energy, and recovery needs.

What Actually Rotates?

When people say “rotate seasonally,” they usually mean the whole pattern, not only one product category.

Drinks

Teas & Infusions

Herbal teas are the easiest seasonal lever because they are simple to switch without changing your whole diet.

  • Spring: ginger, nettle, dandelion, burdock
  • Summer: mint, hibiscus, rose, fennel
  • Fall/Winter: ginger, cinnamon, clove, turmeric
Food

Meals & Texture

Food rotation means shifting from lighter cleansing meals to cooling meals to warm grounding meals.

  • More raw and bitter in spring if digestion feels heavy
  • More hydrating fruits and cooling herbs in summer
  • More soups, stews, roots, and cooked meals in cold months
Herbs

Spices & Botanicals

Stronger herbs are usually used intentionally, while culinary spices can rotate more gently through food.

  • Heating: ginger, cayenne, black pepper, clove
  • Cooling: mint, coriander, fennel, rose
  • Grounding: turmeric, cinnamon, nutmeg, warm oils
Routine

Daily Rhythm

Ayurveda also looks at routines: sleep, movement, oiling, bathing, and timing of heavier meals.

  • Spring: movement, decluttering, lighter meals
  • Summer: cooling, hydration, less overheating
  • Fall/Winter: warmth, consistency, earlier grounding routines

Season-by-Season Guide

Use this as a gentle reference, not a strict rulebook. Your digestion, climate, medications, and health conditions matter.

Spring

Lighter, cleansing, moving

Spring is often treated as a time to reduce heaviness and support digestion, lymph flow, and fresh energy.

  • Favor lighter meals, greens, legumes, herbs, and bitter foods
  • Useful teas: ginger, nettle, dandelion, burdock, lemon
  • Reduce overly heavy, oily, very sweet foods if you feel sluggish
  • Good routine: walking, sweating lightly, cleaning up the kitchen staples

Summer

Cooling, hydrating, calming

Summer rotation aims to cool heat, support hydration, and avoid stacking too many hot spices on top of hot weather.

  • Favor cucumber, melon, leafy herbs, yogurt, citrus, coconut water
  • Useful teas: mint, hibiscus, rose, fennel, coriander
  • Go easier on cayenne, excess ginger, black pepper, and fried foods
  • Good routine: shade, hydration, lighter evening meals
coolinghydratingcalming
Fall

Grounding, warming, steady

Fall can feel dry, windy, and inconsistent. The rotation becomes warmer, more cooked, and more grounding.

  • Favor soups, stews, oats, root vegetables, warm grains, olive oil
  • Useful teas: ginger, cinnamon, licorice, tulsi, turmeric
  • Reduce too much raw/cold food if digestion feels unstable
  • Good routine: consistent meals, earlier nights, warm showers
warminggroundingsteady
Winter

Deep nourishment, warmth, resilience

Winter rotation supports warmth, minerals, immunity, and deeper nourishment without becoming too heavy.

  • Favor stews, lentils, beans, fish, eggs, warm spices, cooked vegetables
  • Useful teas: ginger, clove, cinnamon, turmeric, cardamom
  • Add warming fats: olive oil, tahini, nuts, seeds, avocado
  • Good routine: strength training, warm breakfast, steady sleep

Simple Rule for Your App

Keep the Mediterranean base

Your Aegean Week base can stay the same: olive oil, legumes, fish, chicken, greens, yogurt, grains, fruit, nuts, and seeds.

Rotate the accent layer

Instead of rebuilding the whole meal plan, rotate teas, spices, herbs, sides, and preparation style by season.

Listen to the body

If you feel too hot, cool things down. If you feel sluggish, lighten up. If you feel scattered or cold, warm and ground.

Safety note: Herbs can interact with medications, health conditions, pregnancy, blood pressure, blood sugar, and blood thinners. Treat this as a food-and-wellness reference, not medical advice.
