Teas & Health Benefits

A reference for true teas (from Camellia sinensis) and herbal infusions (tisanes) — what each is traditionally used for, the strength of the evidence behind it, its caffeine level, and the season it tends to suit best. Tea is a pleasant daily ritual and a source of hydration and plant compounds; it is not a treatment or a replacement for medical care.

About "best season": the seasonal pairings here are a blend of traditional practice and practical sense — warming, immune-leaning brews for cold months; cooling, hydrating ones for heat; cleansing tonics for spring; grounding cups for autumn. They are gentle suggestions, not rules. Any tea can be enjoyed year-round, hot or iced.

Best tea by season

A quick seasonal cheat sheet. Think of it as matching the cup to the weather and the body's needs — lighter and cleansing when life wakes up, cooling when it's hot, grounding as it turns, and warming when it's cold.

🌱 Spring

Light, fresh, gently cleansing — supporting the body as it shakes off winter. Allergy season makes nettle and green teas popular.

  • Green tea & matcha
  • White tea
  • Nettle
  • Dandelion
  • Lemon balm

☀️ Summer

Cooling and hydrating, lovely served iced. Mint and hibiscus are refreshing; caffeine-free options keep you hydrated in the heat.

  • Peppermint & spearmint
  • Hibiscus
  • Lemongrass
  • Chamomile (iced)
  • White tea

🍂 Autumn

Grounding, slightly warming, with a little structure for cooler days. Oolong and roasted teas suit the turn of the season.

  • Oolong
  • Black tea
  • Pu-erh
  • Rooibos
  • Cinnamon-spiced blends

❄️ Winter

Warming, comforting and immune-leaning for cold-and-flu season. Spiced and root teas bring heat from the inside.

  • Ginger
  • Elderberry & echinacea
  • Black tea & chai
  • Pu-erh
  • Cinnamon & sage

Several teas are genuinely year-round staples — rooibos, tulsi (holy basil), chamomile and peppermint fit any season depending on whether you serve them hot or iced.

How to read the evidence tags

Traditional Use means a long history in tea cultures and folk practice. Preliminary Research means early human, animal, or lab evidence. Clinical Evidence means human trials or reviews exist, though quality varies. Limited Evidence means the claim should be treated carefully.

Caffeine, simply

Only true teas from Camellia sinensis — green, white, oolong, black, pu-erh, matcha — naturally contain caffeine. Herbal infusions like chamomile, peppermint, ginger, hibiscus, and rooibos are caffeine-free, which is why they suit evenings and sensitive drinkers. Yerba mate, though not a Camellia, is naturally caffeinated.

Safety cautions

Caffeine sensitivity

Green, black, oolong, matcha, and yerba mate can disturb sleep, raise heart rate, or worsen anxiety in sensitive people. Keep caffeinated tea to earlier in the day.

Medication interactions

Tannins in true tea can reduce iron absorption; green tea may interact with blood thinners; hibiscus may lower blood pressure; licorice and others interact with medication. Check with a clinician.

Pregnancy & breastfeeding

Limit caffeine and avoid strong medicinal herbs (e.g. high-dose hibiscus, licorice, some detox blends) unless a clinician approves.

Allergies & quality

Chamomile and echinacea are in the ragweed/daisy family and can trigger allergies. Buy from reputable sources, as herbal blends vary in quality and purity.
