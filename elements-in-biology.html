Elements in Biology

Of the 90-odd elements found in nature, only about two dozen are essential to life — and most of those are needed in vanishingly small amounts. This guide walks through the chemical elements the body depends on, what each one actually does inside a living cell, and the handful of elements whose biology is mostly a story of toxicity.

How to read this page. Elements are grouped by the kind of job they do. The bulk electrolyte elements (sodium, potassium, magnesium, calcium) are needed in gram quantities and run the electrical and structural side of physiology. The essential trace metals (manganese, iron, cobalt, copper, zinc, molybdenum) sit at the catalytic heart of enzymes, usually in microgram-to-milligram amounts. The trace non-metals (selenium, iodine) are built directly into specialised molecules. Finally, arsenic and fluorine illustrate the blurry edge between essential, incidental, and toxic.

Bulk Electrolyte Elements

These four are the most abundant mineral elements in the body. Three of them spend most of their time as simple charged ions whose gradients — sharp differences in concentration across a cell membrane — store the energy that drives nerve impulses, muscle contraction, and the transport of almost everything else.

SodiumNa

The Outside Ion

Sodium is the dominant cation in the fluid outside cells. The sodium–potassium pump (Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase) burns roughly a quarter of the body's resting energy keeping sodium out and potassium in, building the gradient that nearly all other cellular transport borrows from.

  • Sets extracellular volume and blood pressure via osmosis
  • Its inrush is the rising edge of every nerve action potential
  • Powers secondary transport of glucose and amino acids into cells
extracellular cationaction potential
PotassiumK

The Inside Ion

Potassium mirrors sodium: it is the main cation inside cells. The outward leak of potassium sets the resting membrane voltage of every neuron and muscle fibre, and its return repolarises the cell after each impulse.

  • Establishes the negative resting potential of excitable cells
  • Tight blood levels are critical — both highs and lows disturb heart rhythm
  • Balances sodium's effect on blood pressure
intracellular cationheart rhythm
MagnesiumMg

ATP's Partner

Inside the cell, the biologically active form of ATP is the magnesium complex, Mg-ATP. Because of this, magnesium is a required cofactor for more than 300 enzymes — every kinase, the ribosome, and the machinery that copies DNA. In plants it sits at the centre of the chlorophyll molecule.

  • Stabilises the structure of ATP, DNA, and RNA
  • Cofactor for energy metabolism, protein synthesis, and DNA repair
  • Acts as a natural calcium antagonist, relaxing muscle and nerve
enzyme cofactorMg-ATP
CalciumCa

Structure and Signal

Calcium plays a double role. About 99% is locked into the mineral of bone and teeth (hydroxyapatite). The remaining fraction is kept extremely low inside cells precisely so that a brief calcium influx can act as a sharp, unambiguous signal — triggering muscle contraction, neurotransmitter release, and blood clotting.

  • Mineral scaffold of the skeleton and a calcium reservoir
  • Universal second messenger, often read out by the protein calmodulin
  • Required cofactor in the clotting cascade
bone mineralsecond messenger

Essential Trace Metals

These metals are needed in tiny amounts, but life cannot run without them. Almost all work the same way: a single metal atom held inside a protein does chemistry that the protein's amino acids cannot do alone — usually shuttling electrons or activating oxygen. Their ability to switch between oxidation states is exactly what makes them useful, and also what makes them dangerous loose in the cell, so the body chaperones them carefully.

ManganeseMn

Oxygen Chemist

Manganese activates a small but essential set of enzymes: manganese superoxide dismutase (the antioxidant that protects mitochondria), arginase in the urea cycle, and pyruvate carboxylase in glucose production. In plants, a cluster of four manganese atoms in Photosystem II performs the single most important reaction on Earth — splitting water to release oxygen.

  • Core of mitochondrial Mn-SOD antioxidant defence
  • Cofactor for bone formation and carbohydrate metabolism
  • Water-splitting catalyst of photosynthesis
Mn-SODphotosynthesis
IronFe

The Oxygen Carrier

Iron is the workhorse trace metal. Held inside a flat ring called heme, it binds oxygen in haemoglobin and myoglobin; held in the cytochromes and iron–sulfur clusters of the mitochondria, it passes electrons down the respiratory chain that makes most of our ATP. Because free iron generates damaging radicals, the body never lets it roam — it is bound to transferrin in transit and ferritin in storage.

  • Oxygen transport (haemoglobin) and storage (myoglobin)
  • Electron transport via cytochromes and Fe-S clusters
  • Tightly chaperoned to prevent radical damage
hemeelectron transport
CobaltCo

The B12 Atom

Cobalt is unusual: humans need it only as the single atom buried at the centre of vitamin B12 (cobalamin). We cannot use inorganic cobalt at all, so the requirement is met entirely by eating B12, which is ultimately made by bacteria. B12 enzymes handle some of the trickiest chemistry in the body, including rearranging carbon skeletons and recycling folate.

  • Functional only as the cobalt centre of vitamin B12
  • Supports red-blood-cell formation and nerve myelin
  • Ultimately of bacterial origin — found in animal foods
vitamin B12cobalamin
CopperCu

Iron's Co-Worker

Copper sits at the very end of the respiratory chain, in cytochrome c oxidase, where it helps hand electrons to oxygen. It is also needed to load iron correctly (via ceruloplasmin), to cross-link collagen and elastin (lysyl oxidase), and to mop up radicals (Cu/Zn-SOD). In many molluscs and arthropods, copper — not iron — carries oxygen, turning their blood blue.

  • Final electron acceptor step of cellular respiration
  • Required for proper iron handling and connective-tissue strength
  • Oxygen carrier in invertebrate haemocyanin
cytochrome c oxidasecollagen cross-linking
ZincZn

The Universal Tool

Zinc is involved in an estimated 3,000 human proteins. Unlike iron and copper it does not change oxidation state, which makes it a safe, stable workhorse for two jobs: catalysis (as in carbonic anhydrase, which interconverts CO₂ and bicarbonate astonishingly fast) and structure. Tens of thousands of "zinc finger" proteins fold around a zinc atom to grip DNA and switch genes on and off.

  • Catalytic core of hundreds of enzymes
  • Structural anchor of zinc-finger gene regulators
  • Essential for immunity, wound healing, taste, and growth
zinc fingerscarbonic anhydrase
MolybdenumMo

The Sulfur and Nitrogen Handler

Molybdenum works only when slotted into an organic carrier called the molybdopterin cofactor. In humans it powers a handful of enzymes — sulfite oxidase (which detoxifies sulfur), xanthine oxidase (which makes uric acid), and aldehyde oxidase. In bacteria, molybdenum-containing nitrogenase performs the planet's biological nitrogen fixation, converting inert N₂ gas into usable ammonia.

  • Cofactor for sulfite oxidase and purine breakdown
  • Human deficiency is almost unknown from a normal diet
  • Key to bacterial nitrogen fixation that feeds the biosphere
molybdopterinnitrogen fixation

Trace Non-Metals

Selenium and iodine are not metals, and they are not used as loose ions. Instead each is built directly into a specific biological molecule, where its unusual chemistry is irreplaceable — which is also why both have narrow safe ranges.

SeleniumSe

The 21st Amino Acid

Selenium is incorporated into proteins as selenocysteine — a genetically encoded amino acid often called the 21st. Selenoproteins are the body's front-line antioxidants: glutathione peroxidase neutralises peroxides, thioredoxin reductase maintains the cell's redox balance, and the deiodinases activate thyroid hormone. The therapeutic window is narrow; too much causes selenosis.

  • Built into glutathione peroxidase and thioredoxin reductase
  • Activates thyroid hormone alongside iodine
  • Narrow gap between requirement and toxicity
selenocysteineantioxidant defence
IodineI

The Thyroid Element

Iodine has essentially one job in the human body, but it is a profound one: it is the irreplaceable building block of the thyroid hormones thyroxine (T4) and triiodothyronine (T3), named for the three or four iodine atoms each carries. These hormones set the metabolic rate of every tissue and are critical for brain development before birth, which is why iodised salt is one of public health's great success stories.

  • Structural atom of thyroid hormones T3 and T4
  • Governs metabolic rate throughout the body
  • Deficiency causes goitre and, in pregnancy, developmental harm
thyroid hormoneiodised salt

The Blurry Edge: Arsenic & Fluorine

Not every element fits neatly into "essential" or "useless." Arsenic and fluorine are best known as poisons, yet both touch biology in genuinely interesting ways — a reminder that the line between nutrient and toxin is almost always a matter of dose and chemical form.

ArsenicAs

Poison and Puzzle

Arsenic's toxicity comes from mimicry: arsenate looks enough like phosphate to slip into energy metabolism and sabotage it, while arsenite jams enzymes by grabbing their sulfur groups. Yet there are hints arsenic may be a trace nutrient in tiny amounts, and marine life routinely converts it into harmless organic forms such as arsenobetaine — which is why seafood is high in arsenic that passes through us largely untouched. The body methylates and excretes inorganic arsenic as a defence.

  • Arsenate mimics phosphate; arsenite binds enzyme thiols
  • Marine organisms detoxify it into organic arsenobetaine
  • Possibly essential in ultratrace amounts — debated
phosphate mimicarsenobetaine
FluorineF

The Tooth Hardener

Fluorine, as fluoride ion, has no proven essential role in human metabolism — no enzyme requires it. Its biological importance is almost entirely about teeth: fluoride swaps into the enamel mineral to form fluorapatite, which resists acid and decay far better than ordinary enamel. That single property underpins fluoridated water and toothpaste. Living things almost never make carbon–fluorine bonds; the rare ones they do make, like fluoroacetate, are potent poisons.

  • Hardens enamel and bone as fluorapatite
  • No known essential metabolic function
  • Natural organofluorines are rare and usually toxic
fluorapatitecaries resistance

The big picture. Life is built from a handful of organic elements (carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, phosphorus, sulfur) plus this short list of mineral elements. The metals do the chemistry organic molecules can't — moving electrons and activating oxygen — while the bulk ions run the electrical and structural machinery. What makes an element essential is not how much of it there is, but whether biology has built a molecule that cannot work without it.
