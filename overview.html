Vitamins & Minerals That Fight Inflammation
Anti-inflammatory nutrition works best as a balanced food pattern: whole foods first, supplements only when needed, and clinician guidance for individual dosing or health conditions.
NutrientMain anti-inflammatory roleFood-first sources
Vitamin CAntioxidant support that helps neutralize free radicals and reduce oxidative stress.Citrus, bell peppers, broccoli, kale, strawberries
Vitamin DHelps regulate immune activity and supports a healthier inflammatory response.Sunlight, sardines, mackerel, egg yolks, fortified foods
Vitamin EFat-soluble antioxidant that helps protect cell membranes from oxidative damage.Almonds, sunflower seeds, avocado, spinach
MagnesiumSupports immune regulation, stress resilience, blood-sugar control, and calmer inflammatory signaling.Pumpkin seeds, spinach, almonds, legumes, dark chocolate
ZincSupports immune function, tissue repair, and balanced inflammatory response.Beef, shellfish, pumpkin seeds, chicken, legumes
Omega-3 fatty acidsEPA and DHA help resolve inflammation and support heart, brain, mood, and joint health.Salmon, sardines, mackerel, chia seeds, flaxseed, walnuts
SeleniumAntioxidant mineral used in glutathione-related defenses that protect cells from oxidative stress.Brazil nuts, tuna, eggs, sardines, turkey
Use food sources whenever possible. If considering supplements, ask a healthcare professional about appropriate amounts, interactions, and whether testing is useful.
Vitamins & Minerals for Focus
Focus depends on sleep, hydration, blood sugar, movement, and enough brain-supportive nutrients. Use food first; supplements are most useful when intake is low, deficiency is likely, or testing shows a need.
NutrientHow it connects to focusFood-first sourcesSupplement caution
IronSupports oxygen transport to the brain. Low iron can contribute to fatigue, low stamina, and cognitive strain.Red meat, liver, sardines, lentils, spinach, pumpkin seedsDo not supplement without testing ferritin/iron status; excess iron can be harmful.
Omega-3 fatty acidsDHA is concentrated in the brain, and omega-3 intake supports brain and mood health.Salmon, sardines, mackerel, trout, chia seeds, flaxseed, walnutsHigh-dose EPA/DHA can affect bleeding risk and immune response; ask a clinician if on blood thinners.
Vitamin B6Helps make neurotransmitters and supports normal nervous-system function.Salmon, turkey, chicken, potatoes, bananas, chickpeasChronic high-dose B6 supplements can cause nerve symptoms.
Vitamin B12Supports nerve function and red blood cell formation; low B12 can cause fatigue and cognitive symptoms.Fish, beef, eggs, dairy, fortified foodsVegans, older adults, and people with absorption issues may need testing or supplementation.
MagnesiumSupports nerve signaling, muscle function, sleep quality, and stress regulation, all of which affect attention.Pumpkin seeds, spinach, almonds, black beans, dark chocolateHigh-dose supplements can cause diarrhea and interact with some medications.
Vitamin DSupports brain, immune, and mood-related functions; low status may worsen fatigue or low mood.Sunlight, sardines, mackerel, egg yolks, fortified milkTest 25-OH vitamin D before high-dose supplementation; excess can be toxic.
Balanced meals matter more than isolated pills: pair protein, complex carbohydrates, healthy fats, hydration, and minerals to avoid energy crashes that feel like poor focus.
Water-Soluble Vitamins
These vitamins dissolve in water and are not stored in the body — they must be replenished daily through diet.
